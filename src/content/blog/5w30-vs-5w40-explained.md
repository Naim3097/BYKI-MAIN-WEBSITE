# 5W-30 vs 5W-40 — Choosing the Right Viscosity for Your Car

> **Quick Answer:** Both oils flow identically when cold (5W), but 5W-40 is **thicker at operating temperature** than 5W-30. Use whichever your owner's manual recommends. 5W-30 suits most Japanese and Korean cars. 5W-40 is common in European engines and some turbocharged applications. In Malaysia, the choice is purely about engine design — not climate.

## Understanding the Numbers

The "5W" part is identical in both oils — they behave the same way in cold starts. The difference is entirely in the second number:

| Part | 5W-30 | 5W-40 |
|------|-------|-------|
| **Cold viscosity (5W)** | Same | Same |
| **Hot viscosity** | 30 — thinner at 100°C | 40 — thicker at 100°C |
| **Kinematic viscosity at 100°C** | 9.3–12.5 mm²/s | 12.5–16.3 mm²/s |

In practical terms: at operating temperature, 5W-40 creates a **thicker oil film** between moving parts than 5W-30. This doesn't automatically mean "better protection" — it means different engines need different film thicknesses.

- **European engines** often run hotter and have longer service intervals, requiring thicker oil at operating temperature
- **Turbocharged engines** generate more heat in the turbo bearing, benefiting from thicker oil film
- **Higher-performance engines** with wider bearing clearances are designed around 40-weight oil

## The Real Question: Does Thicker Oil = Better Protection?

**No — thicker oil means different protection, designed for different engines.**

### Common myth: "5W-40 is better because it's thicker"
This logic is like saying bigger shoes are better — only if your feet are bigger. An engine designed for 5W-30 has bearing clearances, oil pump capacity, and oil passages sized for 30-weight oil.

### What happens if you use 5W-40 in a 5W-30 engine:
- **Higher oil pressure** — thicker oil meets more resistance in narrower passages
- **Slightly worse fuel economy** — more energy needed to pump thicker oil
- **VVT response may be slower** — variable valve timing solenoids are calibrated for specific oil flow rates
- **Oil bypass valve may open sooner** — excess pressure forces oil through the bypass, reducing filtration

### What happens if you use 5W-30 in a 5W-40 engine:
- **Thinner film at operating temperature** — less cushion on bearings designed for a thicker film
- **Possible increased wear under hard driving** — highway cruising, towing, or spirited driving may stress the thinner film
- **Oil consumption may increase** — thinner oil passes more easily through worn seals and rings
- **Turbo lubrication reduced** — turbocharger bearings spinning at 100,000+ RPM need adequate oil film

## Malaysia-Specific Considerations

### Temperature irrelevance
Both oils have the same cold-flow rating (5W), and both perform identically at Malaysian morning temperatures. The hot viscosity difference (30 vs 40) matters at engine operating temperature (90–110°C), which is the same whether you're in Johor Bahru or Jeddah.

### Driving conditions
Malaysian driving involves a lot of **stop-and-go traffic** (low-stress for oil) mixed with **highway blasts** (moderate stress). Neither condition demands 5W-40 if your engine is designed for 5W-30.

### Turbocharged Malaysian cars
The Proton X50 and X70 with turbo engines are a common discussion point. Check your owner's manual — some X50 variants specify 5W-30, while Proton Malaysia recommends following the specific variant's service booklet. The turbo generates heat, but the engine's oil system is designed for the specified viscosity.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: Can I switch from 5W-30 to 5W-40 for "better protection"?
A: Unnecessary if your engine is designed for 5W-30. You'll spend more on oil and gain nothing — possibly losing a small amount of fuel efficiency. The 30-weight oil provides correct protection for 30-weight-designed engines.

### Q: My engine has high mileage (150K+ km). Should I go thicker?
A: This is a case where switching from 5W-30 to 5W-40 *might* help — if you notice increased oil consumption or hear lifter ticking. The thicker oil can compensate for worn clearances. But this is a graduated decision, not an automatic rule. If the engine runs fine on 5W-30 at high mileage, keep using it.

### Q: The workshop doesn't have my specified 5W-30. Can I use 5W-40 this one time?
A: Yes, one oil change with the adjacent viscosity won't damage the engine. But switch back at the next change. Don't let a one-time convenience become a permanent habit.

### Q: Is 5W-40 better for turbo cars?
A: Only if the manufacturer specifies it. Modern turbo engines like the Honda 1.5 VTEC Turbo use 0W-20 — an extremely thin oil for a turbocharged engine. The turbo oil system is designed for the specified viscosity. Thicker ≠ better for turbos.

### Q: Will using the wrong viscosity void my warranty?
A: Technically, manufacturers can reject warranty claims if engine damage occurred while using non-specified oil. In practice, this is hard to enforce unless there's clear evidence. But why risk it? Just use the right oil.
